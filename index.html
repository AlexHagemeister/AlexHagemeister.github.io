About

I spent years building large-scale physical systems—temple installations at Burning Man, off-grid infrastructure, touring logistics. Coordinating 60-person crews, managing complex timelines, designing systems that had to work under extreme constraints. The work was about emergent order: getting complex groups to accomplish hard things.

I pivoted to software and machine learning because I updated on substrate. Bits scale better than atoms. The fundamental interest stayed the same—systems architecture, high-leverage coordination, tools that amplify capability. I'm drawn to problems where careful design creates order from complexity.

Currently finishing an EECS degree at Berkeley. Recent work includes ML research (co-authored a paper on kernel methods with Prof. Mark van der Laan), education technology (RAG-based AI tutors for Berkeley courses), and automation infrastructure. I care about systems that coordinate complexity, both technical and social.

I'm in an early discovery phase with complexity science—game theory, cybernetics, institutional design, multipolar traps. Understanding why certain configurations persist and others collapse. Not just abstract theory: I want to build frameworks that let groups make better collective decisions.
